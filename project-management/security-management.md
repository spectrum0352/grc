How To Protect Data on Your Devices?

A burglary, lost laptop, stolen mobile phone, or misplaced flash drive — all can happen due to lapses in physical security. But they are less likely to result in a data breach if information on those devices is protected. Here are a few ways to do that:

- **Require complex passwords:** Require passwords that are long, complex, and unique. And make sure that these passwords are stored securely. Consider using a password manager

- **Use multi-factor authentication:** Require multi-factor authentication to access areas of your network with sensitive information. This requires additional steps beyond logging in with a password — like a temporary code on a smartphone or a key that is inserted into a computer.

- **Limit login attempts:** Limit the number of incorrect login attempts allowed to unlock devices. This will help protect against intruders.

- **Encrypt**: Encrypt portable media, including laptops and thumb drives, that contain sensitive information. Encrypt any sensitive data you send outside of the company, like to an accountant or a shipping service.

A company employee receives multiple requests for personal information from different organizations claiming to be associated with their subscription services. Analyze this situation from a security perspective and explain potential risks.

**Security Risks of Multiple Personal Information Requests**

Receiving multiple requests for personal information from organizations claiming to be associated with subscription services is a common tactic employed by cybercriminals in phishing attacks. This situation poses several security risks:

**1. Identity Theft**

- **Data Breach:** The primary risk is identity theft. If the employee shares personal information, it can be misused for fraudulent activities like opening new accounts, making unauthorized purchases, or applying for loans.

- **Financial Loss:** Financial loss is a direct consequence of identity theft, as victims may incur expenses due to fraudulent transactions.

**2. Phishing Attacks**

- **Credential Harvesting:** These requests are often part of phishing campaigns designed to steal login credentials. By providing information, the employee inadvertently aids attackers in gaining access to their online accounts.

- **Account Takeover:** Once credentials are compromised, attackers can hijack accounts, leading to data breaches and financial loss.

**3. Data Breaches**

- **Sensitive Information Exposure:** Sharing personal information increases the risk of exposing sensitive data such as social security numbers, birth dates, and financial details.

- **Data Exploitation:** Exposed data can be used for blackmail, extortion, or selling on the dark web.

**4. Malware Infection**

- **Malicious Links:** Phishing emails often contain malicious links that, when clicked, can infect the employee's device with malware.

- **Data Loss:** Malware can steal personal information, financial data, and other sensitive information.

**Mitigating Risks**

- **Verify the Request:** Employees should always verify the authenticity of requests by contacting the organization directly through a known and trusted channel.

- **Be Wary of Urgent Requests:** Phishing attacks often create a sense of urgency to pressure victims into providing information quickly.

- **Avoid Clicking Suspicious Links:** Employees should never click on links or download attachments from unknown sources.

- **Enable Two-Factor Authentication:** Implementing strong authentication methods can protect accounts from unauthorized access.

- **Regular Security Awareness Training:** Educating employees about phishing attacks and social engineering tactics can help prevent falling victim to these scams.

By understanding these risks and implementing preventive measures, organizations can significantly reduce the likelihood of employees becoming victims of phishing attacks and identity theft.

Physical Security

Cybersecurity begins with strong physical security.

Lapses in physical security can expose sensitive company data to identity theft, with potentially serious consequences. For example:

An employee accidentally leaves a flash drive on a coffeehouse table. When he returns hours later to get it, the drive — with hundreds of Social Security numbers saved on it — is gone. Another employee throws stacks of old company bank records into a trash can, where a criminal finds them after business hours. A burglar steals files and computers from your office after entering through an unlocked window.

How To Protect Equipment & Paper Files?

Here are some tips for protecting information in paper files and on hard drives, flash drives, laptops, point-of-sale devices, and other equipment.

- **Store securely** When paper files or electronic devices contain sensitive information, store them in a locked cabinet or room.

- **Limit physical access** When records or devices contain sensitive data, allow access only to those who need it.

- **Send reminders** Remind employees to put paper files in locked file cabinets, log out of your network and applications, and never leave files or devices with sensitive data unattended.

- **Keep stock** Keep track of and secure any devices that collect sensitive customer information. Only keep files and data you need and know who has access to them.

## Justify Security Spendings

As a CISO how would you justify a security spent to the board of directors**?**

Justifying Security Spend to the Board: A CISO's Perspective

Justifying security spend to the board requires a strategic approach that translates technical complexities into clear business value. Here is a breakdown of key strategies:

**Understand the Board's Perspective**

Before crafting your justification, it is crucial to understand the board's priorities:

- **Financial performance:** How does security impact revenue, costs, and profitability**?**

- **Reputation:** How does security protect the company's brand and customer trust**?**

- **Compliance:** What are the regulatory risks and how does security mitigate them**?**

- **Innovation:** How does security enable business growth and innovation**?**

**Quantify the Cost of Insecurity**

Demonstrate the financial impact of a potential breach:

- **Direct costs:** Loss of revenue, legal fees, forensic investigations, regulatory fines.

- **Indirect costs:** Damage to reputation, customer churn, loss of intellectual property, operational disruption.

- **Opportunity costs:** Missed business opportunities due to downtime or loss of customer trust.

**Align Security Investments with Business Objectives**

Show how security supports the organization's strategic goals:

- **Customer trust:** Strong security enhances customer confidence and loyalty.

- **Competitive advantage:** Robust security can be a differentiator in the market.

- **Risk management:** Effective security protects the company's assets and reduces liabilities.

- **Operational efficiency:** Security measures can streamline processes and improve productivity.

**Use Clear and Compelling Metrics**

Present security performance in a way that is easily understood by the board:

- **Key performance indicators (KPIs):** Track metrics like mean time to detect (MTD), mean time to respond (MTR), and mean time to recover (MTTR).

- **Return on security investment (ROSI):** Demonstrate the financial benefits of security initiatives.

- **Risk reduction metrics:** Quantify the reduction in risk exposure.

**Build Strong Relationships with the Board**

Regularly communicate with the board about security risks and initiatives:

- **Executive summaries:** Provide concise and informative updates on security matters.

- **Board presentations:** Use visuals and storytelling to engage the board.

- **Open dialogue:** Encourage questions and feedback from board members.

**Leverage Industry Benchmarks and Third-Party Validation**

Demonstrate that your security investments align with industry best practices:

- **Security frameworks:** Reference frameworks like NIST Cybersecurity Framework or ISO 27001.

- **Industry reports:** Cite reports from reputable sources like Gartner, Forrester, or Ponemon Institute.

- **Certifications:** Highlight relevant certifications and accreditations.

**Consider a Security Insurance Policy**

A well-structured security insurance policy can mitigate financial risks and reassure the board:

- **Transfer of risk:** Demonstrates a proactive approach to risk management.

- **Financial protection:** Provides coverage for potential losses.

**Example Justification:**

"By investing in advanced threat detection, incident response, and employee training, we can reduce the likelihood of a data breach by X%. This translates to an estimated cost avoidance of Y dollars annually. Additionally, a strong security posture enhances our reputation, attracts customers, and supports our growth strategy."

Remember, justifying security spend is an ongoing process. Continuously monitor the effectiveness of your security investments and adapt your messaging as needed. By effectively communicating the value of security, you can build trust and support from the board.
