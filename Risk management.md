# Introduction

**Core Definition of Risk:**

- **Risk is the potential for harm or loss, representing the likelihood of danger or an undesirable event.** This encompasses various domains, including finance, insurance, safety, health, and environmental science.

- Essentially, risk is the "possibility of threat realization."

**Key Components of Risk (Especially in Cybersecurity):**

- **Threat:** Any potential source of harm to an asset or resource. This includes actions that could compromise the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA triad), as well as non-repudiation.

- **Vulnerability:** A weakness in a system or process that a threat can exploit. It is the absence of a safeguard.

- **Risk Formula:**

  - Risk = Threat × Vulnerability

  - Risk = Probability of Occurrence × Impact of Occurrence

**Cybersecurity Risk:**

- Cyber risk specifically refers to the potential for financial, reputational, or operational damage resulting from cyberattacks, threats, or vulnerabilities.

- It is the possibility of assets being damaged or disclosed within a digital context.

- The impact of cyber risk can be severe, including financial losses, reputational damage, lost productivity, and legal liabilities.

- Factors that influence cyber risk include:

  - IT system size and complexity.

  - Data value.

  - Industry and sector.

  - Organizational cybersecurity posture.

**Risk Management:**

- Risk management is the process of identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks.

- In cybersecurity, this involves:

  - Security awareness training.

  - Access controls.

  - Vulnerability management.

  - Incident response planning.

- A holistic approach is required, that includes the organizations network, supply chains, and ecosystem.

- Cybersecurity risk management must be considered by senior management.

- Risk management is a core function in various sectors, including insurance, safety, and environmental protection.

**General Risk Concepts:**

- Risk can be measured by:

  - Likelihood (probability).

  - Severity (degree of harm).

  - Exposure (number of resources at risk).

- In finance, risk is the possibility of financial loss.

- In insurance, it is the possibility of an event causing a loss.

- In safety, it is the possibility of accidents or injuries.

- In health, it is the possibility of developing health issues.

- In environmental science, it is the potential for environmental harm.

- Risk is the possibility that an event will occur, which will impact an organization's achievement of objectives.

**Information Risk Management Program Objectives and Components**

The primary objective of an information risk management program is to equip organizations with practical risk assessment models and immediate management tools. This program should focus on transferring essential skills related to the core components of a robust risk management process:

- **Risk Assessment Proficiency:** Competently conduct comprehensive information security risk assessments.

- **Asset Management:** Perform accurate asset identification and classification for diverse organizational structures.

- **Threat Analysis:** Identify and classify potential threats, understanding their nature and impact.

- **Vulnerability Assessment:** Identify and classify vulnerabilities based on the organization's specific profile.

- **Control Analysis:** Evaluate existing controls within a selected organization.

- **Likelihood Determination:** Accurately determine the likelihood of risks using both quantitative and qualitative methods.

- **Impact Analysis:** Conduct thorough impact analyses based on business and management requirements.

- **Risk Determination:** Synthesize the above processes to complete a comprehensive risk determination for an organization.

- **Control Recommendations:** Identify appropriate control recommendations, understanding various control types and implementation programs.

- **Documentation:** Develop effective documentation skills to record the results of risk management processes.

- **Standards and Regulations:** Identify and understand pertinent standards and regulations related to information security management.

- **Legal and Public Relations:** Describe the legal and public relations implications of security and privacy issues.

**Program Outcomes and Practical Applications**

Upon completion of the program, participants should be able to:

- Identify critical information assets within their organization.

- Specify and implement effective security controls for diverse systems.

- Specify and implement effective monitoring controls.

- **Return on Investment (ROI):** Demonstrate a positive ROI for security control investments, viewing information security as either an enabling factor (reducing costs, generating revenue) or a protective measure (preventing losses). Security control costs must be proportional to the assessed risk and potential reward.

- **Risk Realization and Security Policy:** Understand that a risk materializes when a threat exploits a vulnerability. Security policies, supported by risk assessments, are crucial for implementing cost-effective security controls. The rigor of these assessments depends on:

  - The organization's threat level and external visibility.

  - The organization's sensitivity to security incidents.

  - Relevant legal and regulatory requirements.

Information security efforts should prioritize deploying "good enough security" rather than striving for unattainable perfection.

- **Formal Risk Assessment:** Organizations subject to regulatory oversight or handling critical information may require more formal risk assessment methodologies.

- **Risk Management Principles:** Safeguarding against all threats is impractical. Modern information security focuses on assessing threats and vulnerabilities and implementing cost-effective safeguards through risk management.

- **Industry Best Practices:** Identifying information assets and applying appropriate security measures based on threat and risk assessments is an industry best practice.

- **Standard Methodologies:** Organizations utilize industry-standard methodologies to assess information value and implement flexible, recommended safeguards.

**Risk Management** is a continuous process of identifying, assessing, mitigating, and monitoring cyber risks to protect an organization's information assets. It is an essential component of cybersecurity, ensuring that organizations are prepared to address potential threats and minimize the impact of cyberattacks.

**Risk Management Ownership**

- Risk management is a management responsibility.

**Risk Management Phases**

- **Security architecture:** Define how to protect information assets.

- **Gap analysis:** Identify differences between current and desired security posture.

- **Risk assessment:** Quantify and prioritize risks.

- **Recommendations:** Develop strategies to address identified risks.

**Risk Management and Assessment Summary**

**Key Concepts**

- **Risk:** The potential for loss or damage due to a threat exploiting a vulnerability.

- **Risk Assessment:** Identifying, analysing, and prioritizing risks.

- **Risk Management:** The process of addressing and controlling risks.

- **Risk Tolerance:** The acceptable level of risk an organization is willing to assume.

- **Residual Risk:** Risk remaining after countermeasures are implemented.

**Risk Treatment Options**

- **Risk Avoidance:** Eliminating the threat or activity.

- **Risk Reduction:** Implementing controls to mitigate the impact.

- **Risk Transfer:** Shifting the risk to a third party (e.g., insurance).

- **Risk Acceptance:** Acknowledging the risk and taking no action.

**Risk Measurement**

- **Qualitative Analysis:** Assessing risks based on subjective judgment.

- **Quantitative Analysis:** Assigning numerical values to risks for comparison.

- **Risk Metrics:** Key indicators used to measure risk levels (e.g., ALE, RTO, RPO).

**Risk Reporting**

- **Clear and concise communication:** Tailoring risk information to different audiences.

- **Visualization:** Using graphs and charts to present complex data effectively.

- **Regular reporting:** Providing updates on risk status and mitigation efforts.

**Challenges in Risk Management**

- **Risk identification:** Identifying all potential risks can be difficult.

- **Risk assessment:** Quantifying risks accurately can be challenging.

- **Resource allocation:** Balancing security investments with other business priorities.

# Risk Management Process

## Risk Evaluation Criteria

To quantitatively evaluate risks, we will use a matrix approach combining two dimensions: Impact and Likelihood.

**Impact** refers to the potential consequences or damage that could occur if the risk materializes. We rate the impact on a scale from 1 to 5, with 1 being minor and 5 being catastrophic

- **Very Low (1):** No significant impact on operations, minor financial loss, minimal or no damage to the organization's reputation.

- **Minor (2):** Some disruption to operations, moderate financial loss, minor damage to the organization's reputation.

- **Moderate (3):** Disruption to operations that can be recovered in the medium term, significant financial loss, moderate damage to the organization's reputation.

- **Significant (4):** Long-term disruption to operations, major financial loss, significant damage to the organization's reputation.

- **Extreme (5):** Permanent disruption to operations, massive financial loss, irreparable damage to the organization's reputation.

**Likelihood** refers to the probability of the risk occurring. We rate likelihood also on a scale from 1 to 5, with 1 being rare and 5 being almost certain.

- **Very Unlikely (1):** The risk event may occur only in exceptional circumstances.

- **Rare (2):** The risk event could occur at some time.

- **Unlikely (3):** The risk event might occur.

- **Likely (4):** The risk event will probably occur in most circumstances.

- **Very Likely (5):** The risk event is expected to occur in most circumstances.

We calculate the risk score by multiplying the impact score by the likelihood score. The resulting score will be on a scale from 1 (lowest risk) to 25 (highest risk). This score will be used to evaluate and prioritize risks.

## Risk Acceptance Criteria

The Risk Acceptance Criteria establish thresholds for deciding which risks are acceptable and which require treatment. This is based on the risk score calculated in the Risk Evaluation Criteria.

- **Low Risk (1-3):** Risks in this category are considered acceptable without an immediate need for mitigation. However, they should be monitored to ensure that they do not increase over time.

- **Medium Risk (4-6):** Risks in this category are considered acceptable without an immediate need for mitigation. However, they should be monitored to ensure that they do not increase over time.

- **High Risk (8-12):** Risks in this level are considered significant and require prioritized attention for mitigation. They pose a substantial threat and require proactive measures to reduce their potential impact or likelihood.

- **Very High Risk (19-25):** Risks in this level are unacceptable and require immediate and aggressive action for mitigation. They pose a severe threat to the organization's operations or objectives.

This chapter provides an overview of the risk management process, which is a fundamental component of any robust information security management system (ISMS). As information security risks are continually evolving, the need for a systematic, repeatable, and consistent risk management process is critical for every organization.

It spans several key stages, including context establishment, risk assessment, risk treatment, risk acceptance, and communication and consultation.

## InfoSec Risk Assessment Process

In this section, we will delve into the Risk Assessment process, a crucial component of risk management that involves the systematic identification, analysis, and evaluation of risks that could potentially impact the organization's information assets and operations.

Risk Identification

- **Identify Assets:** Begin by identifying and documenting all assets that fall within the defined scope of the ISMS.

- **Identify Threats:** For each identified asset, document all potential threats. These could be human (like errors or fraud), natural (like fire or flood), or environmental (like power failure).

- **Identify Existing Controls:** For each threat, identify and document the existing controls in place designed to prevent or mitigate these threats.

- **Identify Vulnerabilities:** Document all vulnerabilities in the existing controls that could be exploited by these threats. This could include weak passwords, outdated software, or inadequate physical security.

Risk Analysis

- **Determine Likelihood:** For each threat-vulnerability pair, estimate the likelihood of the threat exploiting the vulnerability, using the predefined scale.

- **Determine Impact:** For each threat-vulnerability pair, estimate the potential impact if the threat were to exploit the vulnerability, using the predefined scale.

- **Calculate Risk Levels:** Multiply the likelihood and impact for each threat-vulnerability pair to calculate the risk level.

Risk Evaluation

After risks have been identified and analysed, they should be compared against the organization's predefined risk acceptance criteria. This involves the following steps:

- **List Analysed Risks:** Start with a list of all identified and analysed risks, along with their calculated risk scores.

- **Apply Risk Acceptance Criteria:** For each risk, compare its risk score with the predefined risk acceptance thresholds for Low, Medium, High, and Very High risks.

- **Categorize Risks:** Based on the comparison, categorize each risk into one of the four categories (Low, Medium, High, Very High). Risks that are within the organisations risk acceptance criteria can be accepted and do not require further treatment. Risks that are not within them, need to undergo the following steps

Once the risks are categorized, they need to be prioritized to decide which risks should be addressed first.

- **Rank Risks:** Within each category, rank the risks based on their risk scores. Higher risk scores should be given higher priority.

- **Document Prioritized Risks:** Document the prioritized list of risks, along with their categories and risk scores. This list will serve as a guide for the risk treatment process.

## InfoSec Risk Treatment Process

<img src="media/image7.png" style="width:6.71725in;height:1.58347in" />

Determine Risk Treatment Options

The first step in risk treatment is to determine the possible options for treating each risk. There are typically four options:

- **Risk Avoidance:** Completely avoid the activities giving rise to the risk.

- **Risk Reduction:** Implement controls to reduce the likelihood or impact of the risk.

- **Risk Transfer:** Shift the risk to another party, such as through insurance.

- **Risk Acceptance:** Accept the risk and monitor it continuously.

For each risk, consider all these options and determine the most appropriate one based on the organization's risk tolerance and business objectives.

Determine Controls

For risks that are to be reduced, specific controls should be determined. These controls could be technical, organizational, legal, or a combination thereof. The objective of these controls is to reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

**Compare Controls with Annex A**

The controls identified should be compared with the controls listed in Annex A of ISO 27001. This ensures that all relevant controls are considered and nothing is overlooked. If any controls from Annex A are not applied, justification for their exclusion should be documented.

**Produce Statement of Applicability (SoA)**

The Statement of Applicability (SoA) is a key document that lists all the controls from Annex A and describes whether they are applicable and implemented or not. For each control, provide a justification for inclusion or exclusion and describe how the control is implemented.

**Formulate Risk Treatment Plan**

The Risk Treatment Plan (RTP) outlines how the identified risks are to be treated. It includes information about the risk, the chosen treatment option, the controls to be implemented (if any), the expected outcomes, the resources required, and the timeline for implementation.

**Obtain Approval**

The final step in the risk treatment process is to obtain approval for the SoA and the RTP. This typically involves presenting the documents to the top management or a designated authority for review and approval. Once approved, the SoA and RTP guide the implementation of the risk treatment process.

Remember, risk treatment is a continuous process. The effectiveness of the risk treatment measures should be monitored and reviewed regularly, and the SoA and RTP should be updated as necessary

**Quantitative risk analysis**

- It is a risk assessment approach that assigns hard costs to each risk. The approach utilizes mathematical formulas to calculate single-loss expectancy, annual loss expectancy, etc. The method lends itself to communicating risk to management and cost justification, but it is impossible to predict the true cost of future events accurately. Quantitative = the Quantity of Money

- It is based on object of data analysing the sufficiency of controls, and uses some numerical method. Risk auditor should understand that there are other quantitative methods. It is not expected that the auditor would learn all of these advanced techniques, they should know of their existence.

- The probability of an event occurring and the likely loss should it occur are the two fundamental elements of the quantitative method. Quantitative risk analysis makes use of single figure produced from these elements called 'Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE)' or 'Estimated Annual Cost'.

- The two simple models of quantitative risk that all auditors and risk professionals must know: Annualized loss and Likelihood of loss

- This is calculated for an event by simply multiplying the potential loss by the probability. It is thus theoretically possible to rank events in order of risk (ALE) and to make decisions based upon this.

**Gap Analysis**

- A gap analysis is performed to highlight any differences between the organization’s current security architecture and the required security architecture, determined in phase II of the assessment. The output from this analysis will give the reviewer an indication of the residual risk.

**Risk Assessment**

- After the gap analysis has been performed the determined residual risk has to be assessed. This assessment produces a level of risk that is measured by the probability of compromise to the confidentiality, integrity or availability of the designated information system and the data processed on it. Determining the level of risk is completed by comparing the relationship between the threats associated to the residual risks and known vulnerabilities or weaknesses.

**Known Deficiencies**

- Where the assessment of the systems safeguards indicates that they are not able to effectively counter known threats, additional safeguards will be recommended to reduce the risk to an acceptable level. The reviewer will also recommend the type of safeguard required its priority and suggested schedule of implementation.

## What is a cybersecurity risk assessment?

A cybersecurity risk assessment refers to detecting the information assets that are prone to cyber-attacks (including customer data, hardware, laptop, etc.) and also evaluates various risks that could affect those assets. It is mostly performed to identify, evaluate, and prioritize risks across organizations.

The best way to perform cybersecurity risk assessment is to detect:

- Relevant threats in your organization

- Internal and external vulnerabilities

- Evaluate vulnerabilities impact if they are exploited

# Risk Analysis

Risk analysis is the process of determining the potential value of an asset and predicting the likelihood of its loss, considering existing security controls. It is both an art and a science, aiming to understand and mitigate potential threats.

**Risk Assessment Methodologies:**

A structured risk assessment follows these key steps:

- **Asset Categorization, Classification, and Evaluation:** Identifying and prioritizing valuable assets.

- **System Characterization:** Defining the scope and boundaries of the system under analysis.

- **Vulnerability Identification:** Discovering weaknesses that could be exploited.

- **Control Analysis:** Evaluating the effectiveness of existing security measures.

- **Likelihood Determination:** Assessing the probability of a threat exploiting a vulnerability.

- **Impact Analysis:** Determining the potential consequences of a successful attack.

- **Risk Determination:** Calculating the overall risk level based on likelihood and impact.

- **Control Recommendation:** Suggesting new or improved security measures to mitigate risks.

- **Results Documentation:** Recording the findings and recommendations for future reference.

**Core Concepts:**

- **Risk Value:** Calculated as Probability \* Impact.

- **Asset Value (AV):** The worth of an asset.

- **Exposure Factor (EF):** The percentage of asset loss expected from a specific threat.

- **Annual Rate of Occurrence (ARO):** The estimated number of times a threat will occur in a year.

- **Single Loss Expectancy (SLE):** The expected monetary loss from a single occurrence of a threat (AV \* EF).

- **Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE):** The expected monetary loss in a year (SLE \* ARO).

**Risk Analysis Steps:**

1.  **Identifying Risks:** Recognizing potential threats and vulnerabilities.

2.  **Evaluating Risks:** Assessing likelihood, potential damage, and assigning a risk rating.

3.  **Determining Countermeasures:** Conducting a cost/benefit analysis of potential security controls.

**Risk Analysis Models:**

- **Quantitative Analysis:** Uses numerical data and calculations to assess risk (e.g., ALE).

- **Qualitative Analysis:** Uses subjective judgments and descriptive terms to assess risk.

**Quantitative Risk Analysis:**

- Assigns monetary values to risks, allowing for cost justification.

- Relies on mathematical formulas (SLE, ALE) to calculate potential losses.

- Key elements: probability of occurrence and potential loss.

- **Asset Valuation:**

  - Assigning dollar values to assets, considering factors like:

    - Acquisition/Development Cost

    - Management/Maintenance Costs

    - Cost to Protect

    - Value to Owners/Users/Competitors

    - Intellectual Property/Market/Replacement Values

    - Productivity enhancement, operational cost, liability of asset loss, and usefulness.

  - **Internal Value:** Costs associated with internal operations (e.g., retrieval time, maintenance, labor).

  - **External Value:** Revenue or value generated from external sources.

  - **Total Value:** Internal Value + External Value + Total Cost of Ownership.

- **ALE Calculation:**

  - ALE = SLE \* ARO.

  - ALE with Safeguard: Recalculate ALE with updated ARO and EF after implementing controls.

  - **Annualized Cost of Safeguard (ACS):** The yearly cost of implementing a security control.

  - **Safeguard Benefit:** ALE (without safeguard) - ALE (with safeguard) - ACS.

- **Process:**

  1.  Identify Asset Value (AV).

  2.  Identify Threats and Exposure Factor (EF).

  3.  Determine Single Loss Expectancy (SLE).

  4.  Identify Annual Rate of Occurrence (ARO).

  5.  Determine Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE).

  6.  Identify Countermeasures and Recalculate ALE.

  7.  Determine Countermeasure Cost and Benefit.

- **Drawbacks:**

  - Data reliability and accuracy are crucial but often difficult to achieve.

  - Probability is often imprecise, leading to potential complacency.

  - Controls often address multiple interrelated events, making accurate calculations difficult.

**Gap Analysis and Risk Assessment:**

- **Gap Analysis:** Compares current and required security architectures to identify residual risks.

- **Risk Assessment:** Evaluates residual risks based on the probability of compromise to confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

- **Known Deficiencies:** Recommends additional safeguards to reduce risk to acceptable levels.

- **Cybersecurity Risk Assessment:** Identifies and evaluates risks to information assets, including:

  - Relevant threats.

  - Internal and external vulnerabilities.

  - Potential impact of exploited vulnerabilities.

**Key Corrections and Clarifications:**

- "Quantitative analysis - Judgements" is incorrect. Quantitative analysis is based on numerical data, not judgments. Qualitative analysis uses judgements.

- "Qualitative analysis – Main terms" is vague. Qualitative analysis uses descriptive terms and subjective assessments.

- The phrase "Quantitative = the Quantity of Money" is a simplified explanation. It's better to say it uses numerical methods.

- The ARO equation was clarified to ARO = Threat Sources \* Single Likelihood.

Risk analysis is a critical process for protecting valuable assets. It involves identifying, evaluating, and mitigating risks through both quantitative (numerical) and qualitative (descriptive) methods. Understanding the core concepts and methodologies is essential for effective risk management.

**Risk mitigation strategy**

Effective risk mitigation involves a structured approach that combines gap analysis, threat modelling, and a continuous improvement cycle.

**1. Gap Analysis and Threat Modelling:**

- Auditors must conduct thorough gap analyses to identify missing security controls.

- This involves comparing existing security measures against required standards or best practices.

- Threat modelling, including the creation of attack trees, is crucial for understanding potential vulnerabilities and attack vectors.

- These analyses enable auditors and security professionals to determine whether identified gaps should be accepted or mitigated, and to select appropriate control types.

**2. Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) Cycle:**

- The PDCA cycle, derived from quality control methodologies and adopted by standards such as ISO 17799 (ISO 27002), provides a framework for continuous improvement of information security management systems (ISMS).

- **Plan:**

  - Identify and analyze security problems, including threats and vulnerabilities.

  - This phase focuses on understanding the current security posture and defining objectives.

- **Do:**

  - Develop and implement ISMS components, including security controls.

  - Auditors must understand the different types of controls and their appropriate application.

- **Check:**

  - Evaluate the effectiveness of implemented ISMS components and controls.

  - Audits serve as a control mechanism and are used to assess the overall process and its components.

- **Act:**

  - Continuously improve security performance through incremental adjustments.

  - Organizations should strive to enhance security systems while maintaining cost-effectiveness.

## Countermeasure

- Categories of Countermeasures

  - *Technical*: Hardware or software mechanisms, Firewalls, IDSs, etc.

  - *Administrative*: Policies and procedures, Management controls

  - *Physical*: Physically tangible, Guards, fences, CCTV, etc.

- Types of Countermeasures

  - *Deterrent*: Discourages violation of security policy, Fences, trainings, guards, etc.

  - *Preventive*: Stops violations of security policies, Firewalls, IPS, mantraps, etc.

  - *Detective*: Discovers violations of security policies, CCTV, audit trails, motion detectors, etc.

  - *Compensating*: Added in addition to other security controls, Encryption of PII at rest and in transit

  - *Corrective*: Return system to secure state after violation of policy, Terminating malicious activity, patching software, etc.

  - *Recovery*: Extension of corrective controls, but more advanced. Backups, fault tolerance, shadowing, clustering, etc.

  - *Directive*: Directs the actions of subjects. Notifications, escape route signs, procedures, etc.

  - Benefit of Attack \< Cost of Attack

The NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF) is a structured approach to managing information security risk. It consists of the following steps:

# Threat or Vulnerability Assessment

A threat is the potential for a person or a thing to exercise (accidentally trigger or intentionally exploit) a flaw or weaknesses (vulnerability) within an organization. There are several types of threats that my occur within an information system or operating environment Threats are usually grouped into general categories such as natural, human, and environmental, for example:

**Natural Threats**: Storm, Lightning strikes, Tornado, Fire.

**Human Threats:** Computer Abuse, Unauthorized access, Sabotage or vandalism, System Tampering, Spoofing, Terrorism, Fraud, Impersonation and social engineering, Hacking, Negligence, Theft, Falsified data.

**Environmental Threats:** Long term power failure, Chemical leakage, Pollution.

The desired outcome of identifying and reviewing (assessing) threats and vulnerabilities is determining potential and actual risks to the organization. Risk is a combination of factors or events (threats and vulnerabilities) that, if they occur, may have an adverse impact on the organizations. Risk is established by considering the potential impact and likelihood of a vulnerability being exploited by a threat. Risk only exists when threats have the capability of exploiting vulnerabilities. The following formula is used to determine a risk score:

**Risk = Impact x Likelihood**

For this assessment, numeric rating scales are used to establish impact potential (0-6) and likelihood probability (0-5).

# Perform risk assessment on 10 azure virtual machines

To perform a risk assessment on 10 Azure virtual machines, you can follow these steps:

1.  Identify the virtual machines. List the 10 Azure virtual machines that you want to assess. You can do this in the Azure portal or using the Azure PowerShell cmdlet Get-AzVM.

2.  Identify the assets and vulnerabilities. For each virtual machine, identify the assets that it hosts, such as applications, data, and operating systems. Then, identify the vulnerabilities that are present on the virtual machine. You can use a vulnerability scanner to identify vulnerabilities, such as Azure Security Center or Qualys VMDR.

3.  Assess the risk. For each asset and vulnerability, assess the risk of a compromise. Consider the following factors:

    - Impact: How severe would the impact be if the asset were compromised?

    - Likelihood: How likely is it that the asset will be compromised?

    - Remediation effort: How much effort would it take to remediate the vulnerability?

    - Remediation cost: How much would it cost to remediate the vulnerability?

4.  Prioritize the risks. Once you have assessed the risk of each asset and vulnerability, prioritize the risks. Consider the following factors:

    - Criticality of the asset: How important is the asset to your organization?
